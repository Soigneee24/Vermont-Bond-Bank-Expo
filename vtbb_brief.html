White Paper Brief

Infrastructure Investment in Vermont: A Portfolio Review

A three-layer analysis of the Vermont Bond Bank's lending patterns, statewide capital outlay, and peer institution practices — prepared for the Vermont Bond Bank.

01The Problem

Decades of lending data, but no integrated view.

The Vermont Bond Bank had accumulated more than three decades of municipal lending records, but lacked an integrated view of how that lending had been distributed across infrastructure types and borrowers — or how Vermont's overall investment compared with national and regional benchmarks. Without that picture, identifying where lending might be well-aligned with statewide needs, and where there could be room to do more, was difficult.

02Client & Context

The Vermont Bond Bank.

Founded in 1970, the Vermont Bond Bank (VTBB) helps the state's small towns and school districts access long-term debt at lower cost through pooled borrowing. It also administers Vermont's Clean Water and Drinking Water revolving loan funds, giving it a broad role in the state's infrastructure finance system.

$3B+
in tax-exempt and taxable bonds across 107 municipal bond series — meaningful scale for a state of only 640,000 residents.
03What We Delivered

A three-layer evidence base.

The analysis combined VTBB's own historical record with two external reference points, so that findings would not rely on any single data source.

Internal Lending Portfolio 1,641 loans from 1991 – 2025, inflation-adjusted, segmented by infrastructure type, borrower category, and year.
Statewide Benchmark Vermont vs. all 50 states on per-capita capital outlay across major infrastructure categories (FY2022 U.S. Census data).
Peer Bond Bank Comparison VTBB vs. New Hampshire Municipal Bond Bank across FY2022 – FY2024, comparing General Resolution new money issuance.
04What We Found

A balanced picture.

The findings point to genuine strengths in Vermont's recent capital position, alongside several patterns that may warrant further attention from the Bond Bank.

◆ Strengths
  • Ranks 17th nationally in highway investment per capita — above the national median.
  • Leads NH and ME in overall per-capita capital spending among New England peers.
  • Successful energy transition: solar overtook biomass as standard component since ~2018.
◇ Areas Worth Attention
  • Water lending share fell 19.7% → 7.1% from FY2022 to FY2024.
  • School buildings now account for 56.2% of new money issuance.
  • Broadband financing recorded at $0; NH committed $21.75M in FY2022 alone.

05Next Steps

Four areas worth exploring.

Rather than firm prescriptions, the paper offers four directions for VTBB to consider, each grounded in a corresponding finding.

Strengthen Water Infrastructure Lending. Consider enhanced program terms for qualifying water and wastewater projects, complementing SRF programs where eligibility or timing constraints apply.
Monitor Education Lending Sustainability. Incorporate enrollment-trend data into credit reviews to support more informed long-term lending decisions in the school district category.
Support Regional Transportation Consolidation. Explore pooled loan structures that bundle multiple municipalities, increasing scale and coherence of transportation lending.
Assess Broadband Lending Feasibility. Review whether broadband infrastructure lending is permissible under VTBB's existing General Resolution authority before any program design.
